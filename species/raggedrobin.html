Ragged Robin

Silene flos-cuculi

Silene flos-cuculi, commonly known as ragged robin, is a distinctive wetland wildflower found across much of the British Isles and Europe. Named for its characteristic ragged-edged pink petals, this species was once a familiar sight in damp meadows and marshes. However, populations have declined significantly in recent decades due to habitat loss and agricultural intensification, making it increasingly scarce in many regions.

Description

Ragged robin is a herbaceous perennial that typically grows 30 to 60 cm tall. The distinctive flowers are produced from May to July and are approximately 1.5 to 2.5 cm in diameter. Each flower has five bright pink petals that are deeply divided and ragged at the edges, giving the plant its common name. The stems are often flushed with red or purple, and the leaves are lance-shaped and grow in opposite pairs. The plant produces abundant seeds contained in a capsule.

Habitat & Distribution

Ragged robin is found throughout the British Isles and much of Europe, extending eastward to Russia and western Asia. It thrives in damp habitats including marshes, wet meadows, stream sides, and fens. The species prefers neutral to slightly acidic soils that remain moist throughout the growing season. Once common, populations have declined dramatically in the UK due to drainage of wetlands and changes in agricultural practices.

Ecology & Conservation

Silene flos-cuculi is an important source of nectar and pollen for various insects including bees, butterflies, and moths. The flowers are pollinated by a range of insects attracted to the abundant nectar. Due to habitat loss, the species is now considered scarce or absent in many parts of its former range and has become a conservation priority in several regions.

Cultivation

Ragged robin can be grown in gardens with damp to wet soils and a sunny or partially shaded location. It is particularly suitable for bog gardens, beside ponds, or in naturally wet areas. The plant self-seeds readily in suitable conditions and is valuable for creating habitat for pollinators and other wildlife.
