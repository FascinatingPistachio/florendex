Summer Snowflake

Leucojum aestivum

Overview

The summer snowflake is a bulbous flowering plant in the family Amaryllidaceae. Despite its name, it typically flowers in spring rather than summer — the name comes from its similarity to the spring snowflake, which flowers earlier. It is native to central and southern Europe and western Asia, and has been widely naturalised in Britain and Ireland, particularly along riverbanks.

In the UK it is sometimes known as the Loddon lily, after the River Loddon in Berkshire where it grows in large numbers. It is a protected species under Schedule 8 of the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981.

Classification

Kingdom Plantae
Order Asparagales
Family Amaryllidaceae
Genus Leucojum
Species L. aestivum

Description

Plants grow to 30–60 cm tall, producing strap-shaped, dark green leaves. Each stem carries 3–7 drooping, bell-shaped white flowers with a small green spot near the tip of each petal. They flower from March to May. The bulbs are toxic if ingested.

Habitat

In the wild, summer snowflakes are found in floodplain woodland, damp meadows, and along the edges of rivers and ditches. They prefer fertile, seasonally waterlogged soils and can tolerate partial shade. In Britain, the best-known wild populations are along the Thames and its tributaries.
