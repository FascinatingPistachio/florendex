Meadow Cranesbill

Geranium pratense

Geranium pratense, commonly known as meadow cranesbill, is a tall, clump-forming perennial found throughout much of the UK and Europe. Characterized by its vivid violet-blue flowers and deeply divided leaves, it is a common sight along roadsides, in meadows, and grasslands during the summer months. The distinctive seed pods, which resemble a crane's bill, give the plant its common name.

Description

Meadow cranesbill is a herbaceous perennial that typically grows 30 to 80 cm tall. The plant forms attractive clumps of deeply lobed, palmate leaves. The flowers, which appear from June to September, are approximately 2.5 to 3 cm in diameter and are typically violet-blue, though pink and white forms exist. Each flower has five petals with distinctive darker veining. The fruits develop into elongated seed pods with a characteristic crane's-bill-like appearance.

Habitat & Distribution

Geranium pratense is widespread across most of the British Isles and Europe, extending eastward to Russia. It thrives in damp meadows, along roadsides, and in disturbed grasslands. The species prefers well-drained to moderately moist soils and full sun to partial shade. It is particularly common in northern regions and upland areas of the UK.

Ecology & Pollination

Meadow cranesbill is an important source of nectar and pollen for a wide range of bees, butterflies, and other insects. The flowers produce copious amounts of nectar, making them particularly attractive to pollinators. The distinctive seed dispersal mechanism involves the fruits explosively dehiscing, flinging seeds away from the parent plant.

Cultivation

This hardy perennial is easily grown in gardens, preferring well-drained soil and a sunny location. It self-seeds readily in suitable conditions and can become naturalized. The plant is long-lived and relatively maintenance-free, making it an excellent choice for wildflower meadows and cottage gardens.
